How the underwriting works

Six moves from rent roll to return.

The studio above runs this waterfall every month for the full hold. Learn the shape once and every institutional model you ever open reads the same way.

Income

Build the top line

Start from in-place rent × rentable SF, grow it by the annual escalation, then subtract vacancy & credit loss and add other income to get Effective Gross Income.

Expenses

Take out the cost of running it

Taxes, insurance, R&M, management, utilities — some fixed per SF, some a share of income, all escalating. EGI minus expenses (net of reimbursements) is NOI.

Capital

Reserve for leasing & capital

Leasing commissions, tenant improvements, and capital expenditures come out below the NOI line to give Cash Flow Before Debt Service — the property's real cash.

Debt

Layer in the loan

Size the loan off LTV, amortize it at the all-in rate (treasury + spread), and subtract monthly debt service. What's left is Cash Flow After Debt Service to the equity.

Exit

Sell on a forward cap

Capitalize the exit-year NOI at the terminal cap rate, net out sale costs and the loan payoff, and you have your reversion — usually the biggest single line in the whole model.

Returns

Solve for the return

Chain every period's cash flow and solve for IRR, sum distributions over equity for the equity multiple, and read annual cash-on-cash. That's the pitch.

Deal presets

A library you can actually run.

Each preset loads a full assumption set into the model. The default — Ashburn, VA Industrial — is the exact teaching deal from the UREC analyst curriculum. The rest are property-type templates calibrated to realistic going-in metrics, so you can feel how a multifamily deal underwrites differently from an office. Change any input and it's your deal now.
